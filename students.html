Ahh, the resume; nothing makes you feel quite so accomplished, yet so, so inadiquate. Here's five achievable goals to set for yourself so that when it comes time, your resume will rise to the occasion and help you stand out.

1: Leave Out Distracting Graphics

As designers, we're hard wired to make things look "pretty." It only makes sense that our resumes should be a reflection of our capabilities in creating something visually interesting. However, when it comes to resumes, keeping it clean and simple will go a long way. You wouldn't want to be rejected from a company just because they look at your resume and don't agree with your personal aesthetic.

2: Don't Be Democratic in Your Descriptions

Who doesn't love good old fashioned democracy?? Resumes. If you had a great leadership role at a company or on a certain project, make sure to give that more attention and description than your summer job as a lifeguard at the beach.

3: List Concrete Skills

Companies see time and time again skills like "organized" on resumes, and companies know just as well as you do that these skills are just filler for your lack of concrete skill. To avoid beeing seen as incapable, instead of using filler skills say things you actually know how to do, like "front end development" or whip out the tried and true "adobe creative suite."

4: Follow Your Own Rules

The key to making a visually appealing resume that doesn't use too many distracting graphics is to create your own set of typographic rules, and follow them. For example, pick one typeface and stick to it. Choose a set of rules to organize your information, like specific margin and font sizes, and your resume will instantly look more professional.

5: Clear Descriptions

If you've held a position before that has a title worthy of explaining, explain it! Say you were the creative director of a project, or some kind of team leader. Being able to explain the work you do and the responsibilities you had will go a long way in making you stand out from the crowd as a strong contender for internships and job opportunities.

What's the Difference between UX ans UI Design?

UX and UI are different fields of design with desceptively similar names. They're not even real acronyms- UX stands for "user experience" while UI stands for "user interface." Both are incredibly important in creating great products like websites or apps, but while UX design is driven by the user's experience, UI design is driven by creating a particular visual language.
